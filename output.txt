# what new laws did napoleon introduce as a ruler
**Subject:** history
**Date/Period:** 1799–1815 (key legislation: Napoleonic Code enacted March 21, 1804; Concordat of 1801; Bank of France established 1800)
**Key Figure:** Napoleon Bonaparte
**Work/Event:** Napoleonic Code (Code Civil des Français) and broader legal/institutional reforms

## Explanation
Napoleon Bonaparte, as First Consul (1799–1804) and then Emperor of France (1804–1814/15), introduced a sweeping series of legal, institutional, and social reforms that fundamentally reshaped France and much of Europe. His most celebrated achievement was the Napoleonic Code (Code Civil des Français), enacted on March 21, 1804. France's pre-revolutionary legal system had been a chaotic mix of royal edicts, religious law, and feudal custom, varying dramatically by region. Napoleon established a special commission that met over 80 times—Napoleon himself presiding at nearly half the sessions—to draft a unified civil code. The resulting Napoleonic Code standardized laws across France, establishing equal rights for all male citizens, the right to religious dissent, the protection of property rights, and freedom of contract. It also reinforced patriarchal authority within the family, stripped women of individual legal rights, reduced the rights of illegitimate children, and controversially reintroduced colonial slavery. The Code was applied across all French-controlled territories (Belgium, Luxembourg, parts of Germany, Italy, Switzerland, Monaco, and the Netherlands) and became a model for civil codes throughout Europe and Latin America, remaining influential to this day. Beyond the Napoleonic Code, Napoleon introduced the Concordat of 1801, which normalized relations between France and the Catholic Church, granting freedom of worship while keeping the Church under state oversight. He founded the Bank of France in 1800 to stabilize the nation's finances. He reorganized the French educational system, establishing lycées (secondary schools) and a centralized university system. He overhauled the judiciary with lifetime judicial appointments and created the Conseil d'État (Council of State) to draft legislation. He also centralized government administration and reorganized military training and academies. These reforms collectively laid the institutional foundations of modern France.

## Key Facts
- The Napoleonic Code was enacted on March 21, 1804, giving post-revolutionary France its first unified legal system covering civil, commercial, and criminal law.
- The Code granted all male citizens equal rights under the law and freedom of religious dissent, but stripped women of individual legal rights and reintroduced colonial slavery.
- Napoleon negotiated the Concordat of 1801, restoring formal relations between France and the Catholic Church under state supervision.
- Napoleon founded the Bank of France in 1800 to stabilize French finances and reorganized the educational system, establishing lycées and a centralized university.
- The Napoleonic Code was applied across all French-controlled territories in Europe and influenced civil law codes in Latin America, Belgium, Luxembourg, and Monaco, where it still applies.
